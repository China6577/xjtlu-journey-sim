Motivation & Research

The Why: We Chose the Active Track

The XJTLU Journey Simulator was developed under the Active Track because we identified a critical gap in how undergraduate students at XJTLU prepare for their postgraduate applications. Through our research, we discovered that many students feel overwhelmed by the complexity of the postgraduate application process, uncertain about which universities and programs to target, and anxious about managing their time effectively between academic preparation and personal well-being.

We chose the Active Track because it allows us to create an engaging, game-based learning experience that transforms the stressful application process into an interactive journey. Unlike passive information resources, our simulator enables students to experience different scenarios, make decisions, and witness the consequences in a safe environment.

Furthermore, the Active Track enables us to incorporate elements of gamification—such as progress tracking, achievement systems, and multiple endings—which naturally motivate students to engage deeply with the material.

The Gap: Literature & Product Review

Academic Papers Reviewed

[1] Beiter et al. (2015) - Depression, Anxiety, and Stress in College Students

R. Beiter, R. Nash, M. McCrady, D. Rhoades, M. Linscomb, M. Clarahan, and S. Sammut. The prevalence and correlates of depression, anxiety, and stress in a sample of college students. Journal of Affective Disorders, 173:90–96, 2015.

Summary:

This study highlights a alarming trend in higher education: a massive surge in students seeking mental health support (visits up by 231%). It identifies academic performance, pressure to succeed, and post-graduation planning as the top three stressors for college students.

Relevance to your project:

It justifies why your simulator is necessary. You are addressing the exact "post-graduation stress" that academia has identified as a primary threat to student well-being.

[2] Garris et al. (2002) - Games, Motivation, and Learning

Rosemary Garris, Robert Ahlers, and James Driskell. Games, motivation, and learning: A research and practice model. Simulation Gaming, 33:441–467, 12 2002.

Summary:

The authors propose an Input-Process-Output model for instructional games. The core of this model is the "Game Cycle"—a continuous loop of user judgment, behavior, and feedback. They argue that by pairing educational content with game features, we can trigger high levels of engagement that lead to specific learning outcomes.

Relevance to your project:

It provides the "How." This research supports your use of a game cycle (making choices and seeing consequences) as a scientifically proven method to ensure students actually learn the application strategies instead of just reading them.

[3] Ceberio et al. (2016) - Interactive Simulations in Physics Education

Ceberio, M., Almudí, J.M. & Franco, Á. Design and Application of Interactive Simulations in Problem-Solving in University-Level Physics Education. J Sci Educ Technol 25, 590–609 (2016). https://doi.org/10.1007/s10956-016-9615-7

Summary:

This research proves that interactive computer simulations are significantly more effective than conventional teaching methods for developing problem-solving skills. It found that students using simulations tend to adopt "expert-type" procedures (like hypothesis making and result analysis) rather than just memorizing equations. It also reported high levels of student satisfaction with this methodology.

Relevance to your project:

It justifies your medium. It proves that a simulation (your project) is a superior vehicle for teaching complex, high-stakes decision-making (postgrad planning) compared to traditional, static guidance.

Design Alternatives

Alternative A: Linear Stage-based Progression
  • Description: Following a traditional game structure where tasks are completed in sequential phases (e.g., Stage 1 focusing solely on GPA, followed by Stage 2 for IELTS preparation).
  • Drawbacks: This approach is overly mechanical and fails to capture the high-pressure, multi-tasking nature of real-world postgraduate applications, where various requirements must be managed simultaneously.
Alternative B: Text-based Branching Narrative
  • Description: A visual-novel-style interface where players make decisions through text-only menus and dialogue options.
  • Drawbacks: It lacks "spatial presence" and environmental immersion. Players cannot experience the logistical challenges and the physical fatigue associated with commuting between different campus locations.
Final Decision: Option C - Open-map Simulation
  • Rationale: Ecological Validity: In reality, the application process is a multi-tasking journey. The open-map system allows players to freely navigate between the Library, Off-campus Agencies, and Dormitories, accurately simulating real-life time allocation and multitasking.
  • Strategic Trade-offs: By integrating the map with an AP (Action Point) system, players are forced to make critical trade-offs between "Academic Effort" and "Well-being." This choice-driven fatigue effectively mirrors the core dilemma of the postgraduate application experience.
